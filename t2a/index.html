Survey on the Use of Funds Under Title II, Part A
Findings from SY 2015-16

About the Survey

The Elementary and Secondary Education Act, as amended by the No Child Left Behind Act of 2001 (ESEA), provides funds to States and school districts to improve the quality of teachers and administrators in order to raise student achievement. These funds are provided through ESEA Title II, Part A ("Improving Teacher Quality State Grants - Subgrants to LEAs"). Under ESEA, States and districts can use funds for a variety teacher quality activities in any subject area. In 2015-16, Title II, Part A provided States with approximately $2.2 billion for teacher quality reforms.

The Survey on the Use of Funds Under Title II, Part A is administered annually to a nationally-representative sample of 800 school districts. The districts are selected based on district poverty levels and district enrollment. The data collected from the surveys are used to develop national estimates on how school districts used Title II, Part A funds across the various allowable activities as defined in the ESEA, as amended. Data provided by the districts are also used to provide a picture of professional development activities for teachers across the country.

The 2015-16 survey had a response rate of approximately 82 percent. The data from the responding districts were weighted for non-response in order to produce the national estimates detailed below.

Distribution of Title II, Part A Funds

School districts receive the majority of the TItle II, Part A funds. Survey results show that 98 percent of districts received Title II, Part A funding for the 2015-16 school year, with the highest poverty districts and largest districts receiving the bulk of the funds. In 2015-16, the highest poverty districts received nearly half (47 percent) of the funds made available to districts, while the larger districts (those with 10,000 or more students enrolled), received 54 percent.

How are the funds distributed by poverty level?

The highest-poverty districts received nearly half (47 percent) of the total Title II, Part A funds made available to school districts in 2015-16. Poverty data were unavailable for some districts; these districts received approximately one percent of the funds.

How are the funds distributed by district size?

Districts with 10,000 or more students received more than half (54 percent) of the total Title II, Part A funds made available to school districts in 2015-16.

Usage of Title II, Part A Funds

School districts are allowed to use Title II, Part A funds for a variety of activities, including, but not limited to:

  • Hiring teachers to reduce class size
  • Offering professional development in core academic content areas
  • Recruiting and retaining highly-qualified teachers
  • Promoting growth and rewarding quality teaching through mentoring, induction, and other support services
  • Testing teachers in academic areas

In 2015-16, Districts used the majority of Title II, Part A funds for professional development activities for teachers, paraprofessionals, and administrators (51 percent) and to pay for highly qualified teachers to reduce class size (25 percent). Most districts (66 percent) reported using at least some funds for professional development for teachers and paraprofessionals, while 35 percent of districts reported using funds for class size reduction. Thirteen percent of districts used all of their funds for professional development for teachers and paraprofessionals, while 15 percent used all of their funds for class size reduction.

How do districts use the funds?

Overall, the professional development activities for teachers and paraprofessionals received the largest share (47 percent) of Title II, Part A funds made available to districts in 2015-16. Class size reduction received slightly more than a quarter of the funds; together, these uses accounted for more than 70 percent of the funds. Initiatives to promote growth and quality teaching, administration, and professional development for administrators were the next most highly funded activities.

Does usage differ by district poverty level?

Across all district poverty levels, a higher percentage of funds were allocated towards professional development activities for teachers and paraprofessionals than class size reduction. As district poverty level decreases, the percentage allocated towards professional development decreases, while, overall, higher poverty districts tend to allocate less towards class size reduction.

Does usage differ by district size?

In general, as district size increased, the percentage used for professional development activities for teachers and paraprofessionals decreased. In addition, the gap with the percentage allocated towards class size reduction decreased as district size increased; in districts with fewer than 1,000 students, the gap was nearly 37 percentage points compared to about two percentage points in districts with more than 10,000 students.

How are the funds for professional development for teachers used?

Together, English/Language Arts and Mathematics received nearly half (47 percent) of the Title II, Part A funds allocated for professional development for teachers. Science received nine percent, technology received seven percent, and history/social studies received five percent. Nearly a quarter of the funds allocated for professional development were used for other subjects, examples of which included Advanced Placement, Gifted and Talented instruction, positive behavioral interventions and supports, teaching strategies, classroom management, and using data to improve instruction.

How many teachers are funded by Title II, Part A funds?

More than 8,500 teachers were funded by Title II, Part A funds in 2015-16. The vast majority - more than 6,800 - of these teachers were elementary school (K-5) teachers, which make up the majority of teachers funded every year. In comparison with 2011-12, there has been a decrease in the number of teachers funded, although the number funded has varied from year-to-year.

How are the teachers funded distributed by district poverty?

In general, the share of teachers funded increased as district poverty level increased. More than 40 percent of the teachers funded with Title II, Part A funds in 2015-16 were located in the highest-poverty districts. Districts without poverty data accounted for less than one percent of the teachers funded.

How are the teachers funded distributed by district size?

Districts with 1,000 to 5,000 students accounted for more than 40 percent of the 11,000 teachers funded with Title II, Part A funds in 2015-16. Together with districts serving 10,000 or more students, these districts accounted for more than 70 percent of the teachers funded.

How are Title II, Part A funds used for college- and career-ready standards and educator evaluation systems?

In 2015-16, 28 percent of districts reported allocating Title II, Part A funds for allowable activities designed to prepare educators to implement new college- and career-ready standards. Districts allocated over $230 million for these activities, examples of which include grade-level and content area professional development sessions on implementation of the standards, hiring instructional coaches and consultants to provide professional development on the new standards, and aligning curricula with the standards.

In addition, 13 percent of districts reported allocating Title II, Part A funds for allowable activities designed to develop or implement educator evaluation systems. Districts allocated more than $48 million for these activities, examples of which include training on evaluation frameworks, rubrics, and tools; training on the use of student learning objectives; and training on the use of peer observations and feedback.

Professional Development for Teachers in 2014-15

School districts also provided data on professional development for school year 2014-15. These data included the number of teachers participating in various types of professional development, the number of sessions of professional development offered by the district on various topics, and the number of teachers participating in those sessions. Districts reported that a total of 2.5 million teachers taught in the core academic content areas in 2014-15. Of those teachers, 2.42 million, or 97 percent, participated in professional development in 2014-15.

What types of professional development did teachers attend?

In 2014-15, teachers most commonly attended professional development offered in the form of full day workshops; more than 2.5 million teachers participated in full-day workshops. The next most common forms of professional development were professional development offered by coaches, half-day workshops, after-school activities, and weekly learning team sessions.

What topics did professional development sessions cover?

Most commonly, professional development sessions for teachers were focused on core academic content area knowledge. Districts offered more than 275,000 sessions in that area. Districts also offered over 200,000 professional development sessions on effective instructional strategies and state academic content standards.

What topics did teachers most frequently attend?

Although districts most frequently offered sessions on core academic content area knowledge, teachers most frequently attended sessions on effective instructional strategies. More than four million teachers participated in sessions on effective instructional strategies. Teachers also commonly attended sessions; nearly four million attended sessions on core academic content area knowledge and more than three million attended sessions on state academic content standards.

Source: 2015-16 Survey on the Use of Funds Under Title II, Part A
