Teacher preparation providers and programs

In 2016, states reported data on 2,137 teacher preparation providers and the 27,557 teacher preparation programs offered by those providers. The providers and programs were classified by their types: traditional; alternative, IHE-based; and alternative, not IHE-based.

What type of providers and programs are most common?

Most teacher preparation providers were traditional. Out of the 2,137 providers, 1,479 (69 percent) were traditional, while 455 (21 percent) were alternative, IHE-based, and 203 (9 percent) were alternative, not IHE-based.

Similar to providers, most of the teacher preparation programs were traditional. Of the 27,557 total teacher preparation programs in 2016, 19,258 (70 percent) of the programs were based in traditional teacher preparation providers, while 5,158 (19 percent) were based in alternative, IHE-based providers and 3,141 (11 percent) were based in alternative, not IHE-based providers.

Is teacher preparation program enrollment increasing or decreasing?

Overall, teacher preparation program enrollment has been decreasing since AY 2009–10. The most substantial decrease was between AY 2011–12 (622,401 individuals enrolled) and AY 2012–13 (500,506 individuals enrolled), when enrollment in teacher preparation programs decreased by 20 percent. Between AY 2013-14 and AY 2014-15, teacher preparation program enrollment decreased by 8 percent, with 418,373 individuals enrolled in AY 2014-15 compared to 455,328 in AY 2013-14.

Are teacher preparation program enrollees primarily male or female?

Between AY 2009-10 and AY 2014-15, teacher preparation program enrollees have been primarily female - about 75 percent of enrollees are female. In AY 2014-15, the number of enrollees who reported their gender as female was 313,758, compared to 99,630 who reported their gender as male.

In comparison with traditional programs, however, alternative programs enroll more males. In AY 2014-15, 34 percent of enrollees in alternative programs who reported their gender were male, compared to 22 percent of the enrollees in traditional programs who reported their gender.

What is the racial/ethnic makeup of teacher preparation program enrollees?

In AY 2014–15, among individuals enrolled in teacher preparation programs who reported their race/ethnicity, 70 percent (276,018) identified as White, 13 percent (52,764) identified as Hispanic, and 9 percent (36,472) identified as Black or African American. In AY 2013–14, 72 percent (304,363) of individuals enrolled in teacher preparation programs who reported their race/ethnicity identified as White, 12 percent (50,973) identified as Hispanic, and 10 percent (42,844) identified as Black or African American.

Is enrollment in teacher preparation programs becoming more diverse?

Based on the individuals enrolled in teacher preparation programs who reported their race/ethnicity, it appears that the field of prospective teachers has become increasingly more diverse over the past five years. The percentage of minority enrollment in teacher preparation programs has increased every year since AY 2010-11; in AY 2014-15, the percentage reached 30 percent.

Does enrollment of males and minorities differ by program type?

Overall, alternative teacher preparation programs enrolled higher percentages of males and minorities compared to traditional programs. In comparison, alternative programs are more spread out, with less clustering around lower percentages of male and minority enrollment.
