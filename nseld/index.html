Appendix B
District Contexts and Teacher Background

Study Design

Surveys were administered to three types of districts differentiated by the level of representation of EL students in the district. The districts were classified as follows:

  • Significant EL-student representation: > 1,000 EL students and > 10% of total district enrollment
  • Moderate EL-student representation: > 1,000 EL students and â‰¤ 10% of total district enrollment OR 101 to 1,000 EL students
  • Low-incidence EL-student representation: 100 or fewer EL students

A total of 767 districts responded to the survey. In addition, 706 teachers who were either mainstream teachers of EL students or EL specialists were surveyed.

The responding districts were generally evenly split between significant, moderate, and low EL-student representation.

More than 60 percent of the teachers responding were mainstream teachers of EL students.

Districts: Background

The vast majority of districts responding - 96 percent of all districts - to the survey have had EL students for more than five years, and more than three quarters - 77 percent - reported offering English as a second language (ESL) instruction.

Districts also responded to various questions regarding their EL student population, including questions on the number of language groups represented in their district and the most common language backgrounds of their EL students, and what types of ELs they enrolled.

Regardless of district EL representation, the most common language background of ELs was Spanish.

More than 60 percent of districts with significant EL representation reported having 10 or more language groups represented in their district.

Districts with significant EL representation more commonly reported having ELs that include refugees and other immigrants who arrived in the United States within the past five years than districts with lower EL representation.

Teachers: Responsibilities

Teachers provided information on their primary roles and the subject areas they taught in. Data from the survey reveal that mainstream teachers of ELs and EL specialists differed in both their primary roles and the subject areas they taught in.

Mainstream teachers of ELs and EL specialists differed greatly in terms of their primary roles. Almost all of the mainstream teachers of ELs taught a mainstream class, while the primary roles of EL specialists varied greatly.

EL specialists more commonly reported that they taught language arts and English as a second language, while mainstream teachers of ELs more commonly reported teaching math, science, and social studies or social science.

Teachers: Qualifications

We also asked teachers about their qualifications and level of education. The survey data show that certifications for mainstream teachers of ELs and EL specialists differed, as did the highest level of education.

EL specialists were, as expected, much more likely to hold ESL certification and bilingual certifications than mainstream teachers of ELs. In contrast, EL specialists were less likely to hold grade level and subject area certifications.

EL specialists more commonly reported having completed a graduate degree - either a master's degree or a doctoral degree - than mainstream teachers of ELs, who most commonly reported having a bachelor's degree.

Teachers: Teaching ELs and Using DLRs

Both types of teachers were asked to provide data on their experience teaching ELs and their experiences with the use of digital learning resources (DLRs). The data reveal that EL specialists, unsurprisingly, teach more ELs, and are more confident in teaching ELs.

EL specialists, on average, taught higher numbers of EL students than mainstream teachers of ELs; on average, they taught 18 more ELs, and the median number of ELs taught was higher by 13.

EL specialists more commonly reported having taught ELs for six or more years, feeling very confident in their ability to teach ELs, and having some use of EL students' language. Both groups of teachers were similar in the percentage using DLRs for six or more years and feeling very comfortable using DLRs in instruction.
