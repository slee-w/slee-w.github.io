Appendix C
District Contexts and Teacher Background

 

Districts: Contexts

The vast majority of districts responding - 96 percent of all districts - to the survey have had EL students for more than five years, and more than three quarters - 77 percent - reported offering English as a second language (ESL) instruction.

Districts also responded to various questions regarding their EL student population, including questions on the number of language groups represented in their district and the most common language backgrounds of their EL students, and what types of ELs they enrolled.

Regardless of district EL representation, the most common language background of ELs was Spanish.

More than 60 percent of districts with significant EL representation reported having 10 or more language groups represented in their district.



Notes: These charts present weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 11 districts (unweighted).
Source: NSELD District Survey (Item 4). N = 767 districts.

Districts with significant EL representation more commonly reported having ELs that include refugees and other immigrants who arrived in the United States within the past five years than districts with lower EL representation.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 12 districts (unweighted).
Source: NSELD District Survey (Item 6). N = 767 districts.

Teachers: Responsibilities

Teachers provided information on their primary roles and the subject areas they taught. Data from the survey reveal that mainstream teachers of ELs and EL specialists differed in both their primary roles and the subject areas they taught.

Mainstream teachers of ELs and EL specialists differed greatly in terms of their primary roles. Almost all of the mainstream teachers of ELs taught a mainstream class, while the primary roles of EL specialists varied greatly.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 4 teachers (unweighted).
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 2). N = 706 teachers.

EL specialists more commonly reported that they taught language arts and English as a second language, while mainstream teachers of ELs more commonly reported teaching math, science, and social studies or social science.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. There are no missing cases; all respondents completed this item. Percentages add to greater than 100 percent since teachers may teach more than one subject area.
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 3). N = 706 teachers.

Teachers: Qualifications

We also asked teachers about their qualifications and level of education. The survey data show that certifications for mainstream teachers of ELs and EL specialists differed, as did the highest level of education.

EL specialists were, as expected, much more likely to hold ESL certification and bilingual certifications than mainstream teachers of ELs. In contrast, EL specialists were less likely to hold grade level and subject area certifications.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 2 teachers (unweighted). Percentages add to more than 100 percent since teahcers may hold more than one type of certification.
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 8). N = 706 teachers.

EL specialists more commonly reported having completed a graduate degree - either a master's degree or a doctoral degree - than mainstream teachers of ELs, who most commonly reported having a bachelor's degree.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 2 teachers (unweighted).
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 9). N = 706 teachers.

Teachers: Teaching Experience

Both types of teachers were asked to provide data on their experience teaching, and, more specifically, their experience in teaching ELs. The data reveal that EL specialists are slightly more experienced in teaching than the mainstream teachers of ELs responding to the survey, and that they, unsurprisingly, are more likely to have taught ELs for a greater number of years than mainstream teachers of ELs.

EL specialists reported a slightly higher mean number of years teaching at the K-12 level, at their current grade level, and in their district than mainstream teachers of ELs.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was from 25 to 26 teachers (unweighted). Estimates are based on teachers who indicated 2016-17 was not their first year of teaching.
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 11). N = 661 teachers.

EL specialists reported having more experience in instructing EL students than mainstream teachers of ELs - nearly 70 percent reported having taught ELs for six or more years.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 3 teachers (unweighted).
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 17). N = 706 teachers.

Teachers: Teaching ELs

Respondents to the teacher survey also provided data on the number of EL students taught, as well as their level of confidence in their ability to teach EL students. EL specialists reported teaching greater numbers of ELs than mainstream teachers of ELs, and had greater confidence in their ability to teach ELs.

EL specialists reported teaching greater numbers of ELs than mainstream teachers of ELs; on average, EL specialists reported teaching 18 more ELs than mainstream teachers of ELs.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 7 teachers (unweighted).
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 4). N = 706 teachers.

EL specialists more frequently reported being very confident in their ability to instruct ELs than mainstream teachers of ELs; no EL specialists said they were not confident in their ability to teach ELs.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 4 teachers (unweighted).
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 15). N = 706 teachers.

Teachers: Technology Training and DLRs

Both types of teachers provided data on the type of training received in professional certification coursework related to the use of technology in instruction, and their comfort and experience in using DLRs in instruction. The types of trainings received and level of comfort with DLRs were similar between both types of teachers, but experience in using DLRs differed.

Both mainstream teachers of ELs and EL specialists provided similar responses regarding the types of training received on the use of technology in instruction.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 79 teachers (unweighted). Responses may total to more than 100 percent since teachers may receive more than one type of training.
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Item 13). N = 706 teachers.

Both EL specialists and mainstream teachers of ELs reported similar levels of comfort using DLRs, but EL specialists more frequently reported having used DLRs in instruction for more than one year.



Notes: This chart presents weighted estimates. Item nonresponse was 3 teachers for item 14 and 4 teachers for item 18 (unweighted).
Source: NSELD Teacher Survey (Items 14 and 18). N = 706 teachers.
